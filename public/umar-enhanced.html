Birth and Early Life

Umar ibn al-Khattab was born in 584 CE in Mecca. He belonged to the Banu Adi clan of the Quraysh tribe. Known for his strength, intelligence, and leadership qualities, he initially opposed Islam and was a fierce enemy of the Prophet. He was a skilled administrator and merchant, known for his physical strength and commanding presence.

Embracing Islam

Umar's conversion to Islam in 616 CE was a significant event that strengthened the Muslim community. After reading verses from the Quran, he was deeply moved and immediately went to the Prophet to declare his faith. His conversion gave Muslims the confidence to pray openly at the Kaaba for the first time. He became one of the Prophet's closest companions and advisors.

Contributions to Islam

As the second Caliph (634-644 CE), Umar established the Islamic calendar, created the first welfare state, and expanded the Muslim empire significantly. He was known for his justice and administrative genius, establishing systems for governance, taxation, and social welfare. He personally patrolled the streets at night to ensure the welfare of his subjects. Under his rule, Jerusalem was conquered peacefully, and he personally received the keys to the city.

Passing

Umar was assassinated in 644 CE by a Persian slave named Abu Lu'lu'a while leading the dawn prayer in the Prophet's Mosque in Medina. Before his death, he appointed a council of six companions to choose his successor. He was buried next to the Prophet Muhammad and Abu Bakr in the Prophet's Mosque. His ten-year caliphate is remembered as a golden age of Islamic expansion and justice, earning him the title "Al-Farooq" (The Distinguisher between right and wrong).
